# Writing Examples

Verbatim and lightly-edited examples from the Manager's Playbook and Officers' Impact Playbook. Use these as the source of truth when showing a manager or officer what "good" looks like, when grading their drafts, or when illustrating a concept (3S Principle, Outcome–Action–Impact, Activity Trap).

When you quote from these examples, attribute them to "the Playbook" rather than fabricating new ones — the user will recognise the language and trust the response more.

---

## 1. Weak vs. Strong Write-Up Pairs

These are the canonical six pairs. Each pair shows the same achievement written badly (Activity Trap) and well (Outcome–Action–Impact).

### Pair 1 — Project management

**Weak:** "Conducted weekly team meetings and managed project timelines."

**Strong:** "Delivered project 2 weeks ahead of schedule by implementing structured weekly reviews and proactive risk management."

Why the strong version works: the activity ("weekly meetings") is now subordinate to the outcome ("2 weeks ahead of schedule") and the action that produced it is named ("structured weekly reviews and proactive risk management").

### Pair 2 — Leadership

**Weak:** "Demonstrated strong leadership skills and effective communication."

**Strong:** "Unified 3 competing stakeholder groups by facilitating structured workshops, resulting in unanimous agreement on project direction."

Why: "Strong" and "effective" are adjectives the panel cannot verify. Naming three groups, the mechanism (workshops), and the outcome (unanimous agreement) is verifiable.

### Pair 3 — Big claim, no attribution

**Weak:** "Led the project that saved the organisation millions."

**Strong:** "Contributed key technical expertise to team effort that reduced system downtime by 50%, specifically designing the monitoring framework that enabled early issue detection."

Why: the weak version overclaims and is vague. The strong version is honest about being a team effort while making the officer's specific contribution visible.

### Pair 4 — Generic helping

**Weak:** "Helped with the project that improved efficiency."

**Strong:** "Led the process redesign initiative that increased team efficiency by 30%, personally conducting stakeholder interviews and designing the new workflow."

Why: "Helped" hides the officer. Naming what they led, what they did personally, and the percentage gain makes the contribution visible.

### Pair 5 — BAU work

**Weak:** "Maintained production systems and responded to user support requests."

**Strong:** "Maintained 99.8% system uptime for critical citizen services platform serving 100,000+ daily users, while implementing automated monitoring that reduced incident response time by 70% and prevented 15 potential outages."

Why: BAU is the most commonly under-written category. The strong version uses Prevention Impact framing — counting outages prevented, not just tickets closed.

### Pair 6 — Failed experiment

**Weak:** "Led 6-month experiment testing blockchain for secure document sharing, which revealed technical limitations and compliance challenges, saving organisation from unsuitable technology investment."

**Strong:** "Designed and executed 3-month pilot testing blockchain for secure document sharing, achieving 85% accuracy improvement over manual process. Experiment revealed critical compliance limitations, saving organisation from unsuitable technology investment and informing revised digital security strategy."

Why: even the "weak" here is decent — but the stronger version (a) shows what the experiment achieved before being killed, (b) names the strategic consequence (revised digital security strategy), and (c) frames a negative result as deliberate learning rather than a failure.

## 2. Three Sample Evaluations

These are three role-and-level samples adapted from the Playbook, restructured to follow the APA six-section schema: **Impact**, **Craft and Execution**, **Ownership**, **Strategic Alignment**, **Culture and Organisational Influence**, **Career Focus and Next Steps**. Each performance statement should be Simple, Sharp, and Succinct and follow the Outcome–Action–Impact structure. When a user asks "what should a write-up at my level look like", offer the relevant one.

### Sample A — Product Manager, Level 2

**Impact**
- Launched the Scheduled Payments feature on the original target date to 50,000+ eligible citizens by defining the MVP scope through a user-value-versus-technical-complexity analysis, resulting in 35% first-quarter adoption and a visible drop in recurring payment enquiries to the call centre.
- Reduced first-time setup error rates by 40% in the first post-launch release by running a data-driven review of adoption metrics and reprioritising two usability fixes (simplified date selection, clearer confirmation messages), resulting in a higher share of users completing setup without support.
- Prevented a two-month slip by proactively identifying a payment gateway API dependency risk and leading cross-team technical discussions that produced a shared timeline and joint testing plan, resulting in the team hitting the original go-live date with no scope reduction.

**Craft and Execution**
- Accelerated engineering velocity across the 3-month build by authoring a comprehensive PRD that translated complex user needs and policy constraints into over 40 clearly defined user stories with explicit acceptance criteria, resulting in minimal rework and a clean hand-off at each sprint boundary.
- Enabled evidence-based post-launch decisions by creating a metrics dashboard tracking adoption and conversion funnels, resulting in the team shipping three data-informed improvements in the first quarter after launch.
- Protected core launch scope under delivery pressure by making a clean trade-off to de-scope a non-essential feature, resulting in the primary user value landing on time at target quality.

**Ownership**
- Owned the feature end-to-end from vision through post-launch monitoring, including personally triaging initial user feedback and synthesising findings for engineering, resulting in the first three production issues being closed within 48 hours of launch.
- Extended scope beyond the assigned PRD by initiating the cross-agency API alignment when no-one else was driving it, resulting in a working partnership with the payments team that has carried into subsequent releases.
- Took responsibility for the cross-functional team of 7 (5 Engineers, 1 Designer, 1 Content Strategist) by facilitating all sprint planning, reviews, and retrospectives, resulting in a steady delivery cadence with no missed sprint commitments.

**Strategic Alignment**
- Secured roadmap priority for the feature by developing a business case that forecasted a higher ROI than a competing proposal, resulting in the other proposal being deferred and this feature commencing on schedule.
- Aligned feature delivery to the agency's citizen-self-service strategic objective by mapping feature outcomes to strategic KPIs and presenting progress to senior stakeholders, resulting in the feature being cited in the division's quarterly strategy review.

**Culture and Organisational Influence**
- Embedded a data-driven sprint discipline in the team by introducing a "Customer Feedback Review" session at the start of each sprint, resulting in a persistent feedback loop between engineering and user needs that outlasted the launch.
- Propagated the user feedback framework to an adjacent product team by walking their PM through the process end-to-end, resulting in that team adopting a similar review cadence the following quarter.

**Career Focus and Next Steps**
- *Move from executing a given roadmap to defining strategy in ambiguous problem space.* Concrete next step: take ownership of the "User Onboarding and Activation" problem space and deliver a Problem Brief based on discovery research by end of Q3. Manager will provide weekly coaching and introductions to two senior PMs who have led comparable discovery work.
- *Elevate data use from feature-level to portfolio-level strategic decisions.* Concrete next step: develop a "State of the Product" quarterly review proposing the top 2–3 strategic priorities for the following quarter's roadmap, with the first edition due by end of Q4.
- *Scale informal influence into formal mentorship and become a force multiplier.* Concrete next step: formally mentor the incoming L1 Product Manager, using the Scheduled Payments project as a case study to teach backlog management and data-informed trade-offs, starting in their first month.

### Sample B — Software Engineer, Level 1

**Impact**
- Saved the team an estimated 3 hours of manual effort per week by proactively identifying a recurring task and, with guidance from the tech lead, building a Python script that aggregates deployment logs and posts a summary to the team's Slack channel, resulting in a durable reduction in weekly overhead.
- Reduced back-end validation errors on a new form by writing 5+ complex JavaScript validation functions that enforced data integrity before submission, resulting in cleaner downstream data and a smoother user experience.
- Resolved a long-running authentication bug within a single sprint by pairing with a senior engineer to analyse system logs, isolate the root cause, and deliver a well-tested fix, resulting in a measurable drop in the team's on-call paging volume.

**Craft and Execution**
- Achieved 95% test coverage on the new form's validation logic by writing a comprehensive suite of unit tests, resulting in zero validation-related production bugs in the six weeks after launch.
- Prevented downstream defects by proactively identifying several edge cases in the requirements while building the form, documenting them, and seeking clarification from the Product Manager, resulting in the spec being tightened before build rather than patched after.
- Consistently delivered high-quality, well-documented code across all assigned tasks, regularly passing code reviews with only minor feedback, resulting in a reliable sprint-by-sprint contribution the senior engineers could plan around.

**Ownership**
- Took full ownership of the reporting automation task from initial proposal through development, deployment, and user feedback, resulting in a tool the team adopted as part of their weekly workflow.
- Extended scope beyond the assigned authentication bug fix by writing up the root cause and solution for the team knowledge base, resulting in the fix being reusable by engineers who hit the same class of issue on other services.

**Strategic Alignment**
- Supported the team's engineering-efficiency goal through the reporting automation task, resulting in reduced manual overhead and capacity freed for higher-value work.
- Safeguarded downstream business processes by focusing on the quality of the form's validation logic, resulting in cleaner data flowing into dependent systems.

**Culture and Organisational Influence**
- Secured the long-term utility of the automation script by authoring and sharing a short usage guide, resulting in the script being maintainable by teammates beyond the original author.
- Strengthened the team's knowledge base by documenting the authentication bug root cause and solution, resulting in faster diagnosis for engineers encountering similar issues later in the year.

**Career Focus and Next Steps**
- *Deepen technical expertise beyond feature implementation.* Focus on the underlying architecture, front-end performance optimisation, and advanced state management patterns. Concrete next step: complete the internal Advanced React Performance course and apply the learnings by leading the refactor of one existing non-critical component.
- *Increase product ownership — understand the "why" behind features, not just the "how".* Concrete next step: actively participate in at least one technical design discussion for a major new feature, with the goal of contributing a concrete idea or identifying a specific risk.
- *Develop system-level thinking across services, the CI/CD pipeline, and cloud infrastructure.* Concrete next step: lead a small, self-contained feature from technical breakdown to deployment, with guidance from a senior engineer, to practise end-to-end ownership.

### Sample C — Designer, Level 3

**Impact**
- Projected $50k in annual operational savings by redesigning the permit application into a simplified, multi-step flow grounded in 15 user interviews and multiple usability tests, resulting in a forecast 15% reduction in call centre volume on application queries.
- Shifted the organisation's framing from "more information" to "simplified actions" by conducting in-depth ethnographic research with 20+ business owners and synthesising the findings into a detailed journey map, resulting in the strategic brief for the next programme of work being rewritten around simplified actions.
- Enabled seamless adoption of new design system components by other teams by authoring the official documentation and usage guidelines, resulting in two adjacent teams picking up the components without design support in the following quarter.

**Craft and Execution**
- Secured leadership buy-in for a new service concept by developing and testing a low-fidelity prototype early in discovery, resulting in the concept being greenlit at the quarterly portfolio review.
- Validated the final design before a single line of code was written by building high-fidelity interactive prototypes and running a rigorous usability testing plan with measurable success metrics, resulting in only minor refinements needed during development.
- Elevated design quality across the team by introducing automated accessibility testing tools (e.g., Axe) into the design hand-off process, resulting in 25+ accessibility issues caught and fixed before they reached development.

**Ownership**
- Drove the permit application project from an ambiguous problem statement to a successfully launched product by managing expectations across three agencies and navigating complex technical constraints without senior oversight, resulting in a launch that held its scope and timeline despite cross-agency friction.
- Took full ownership of the design system contribution — designed the components, drove the review and approval process, wrote the documentation, and presented the new patterns at a cross-team sharing session — resulting in a reusable asset that did not depend on anyone else to complete.

**Strategic Alignment**
- Won executive sponsorship for the workflow redesign by translating user research insights into a compelling business case, demonstrating to product leadership how improved user experience would produce significant operational cost savings, resulting in the project being resourced as a strategic initiative.
- Aligned the design system work to the organisation's broader efficiency goals by framing it as a force multiplier that would accelerate future development and reduce long-term maintenance costs, resulting in sustained investment across the following two quarters.

**Culture and Organisational Influence**
- Contributed to a Level 1 designer's successful growth by mentoring them through the permit application project, providing regular constructive feedback and guiding their first solo user interview, resulting in the junior designer running their own research sessions confidently by year-end.
- Improved the team's design culture by establishing and facilitating a weekly Design Critique session, resulting in a standing forum for designers to share work-in-progress and receive structured constructive feedback.

**Career Focus and Next Steps**
- *Expand influence from leading a single complex project to shaping design strategy for an entire product area.* Concrete next step: partner with the Head of Product for the Citizen Services portfolio to define the 12-month design vision and roadmap, moving from project-level to programme-level strategic role.
- *Evolve from contributing to the design system to leading its governance and strategic direction.* Concrete next step: take ownership of the Q4 roadmap for the design system, with a specific focus on a more streamlined contribution process for other teams.
- *Formalise the Design Critique practice into a recognised team ritual with tracked value.* Concrete next step: develop a formal charter for the Design Critique practice and track key metrics (participation rate, number of projects reviewed) to demonstrate its value to design leadership.

## 3. Sample Promotion Case — Sarah Chen (Grade I → Grade J)

This is the verbatim promotion case the Manager's Playbook ships as its worked example. Use it when the user is preparing a promotion case, reviewing one, or asking what a strong promotion summary looks like.

**Officer:** Sarah Chen — Product Manager, GovTech LifeSG
**Promotion:** Grade I → Grade J · Senior Product Manager

### Promotion Case Summary

Sarah already operates at the next level through multi-agency initiative leadership, strategic product development, and delivery of complex products serving hundreds of thousands of citizens.

### Major Achievements (past 2 years)

**LifeSG Benefits Calculator**
- Led end-to-end development serving 400,000+ citizens monthly
- Coordinated with 8 government agencies, integrated 15+ benefit schemes
- Reduced citizen application time by 60%, increased benefit uptake by 35%
- Generated $12M in additional benefits claimed

**Cross-Product Integration Initiative**
- Spearheaded identity verification integration across 5 LifeSG services with GovTech's National Digital Identity team
- Managed multi-agency stakeholder alignment
- Improved completion rates by 25%

**Product Analytics Framework**
- Established comprehensive analytics across 12 services
- Implemented improvements increasing completion rates by 18%

**Strategic Leadership**
- Defined LifeSG product roadmap aligned with Smart Nation 2030
- Led quarterly business reviews with Permanent Secretary level stakeholders
- Mentored 2 junior PMs, established team best practices

### Gaps Analysis

- **Strategic thinking and planning:** limited experience in multi-year roadmap planning and cross-portfolio prioritisation beyond immediate product scope.
- **Cross-functional influence and stakeholder management:** influence primarily within product area rather than across broader organisation and product management community.

### Mitigation Plans

- Include in LifeSG annual strategic planning process with ownership of 2-year product vision
- Lead cross-product working group for healthcare services expansion
- Mentor 2 new PM hires, establish PM guild practices
- Join GovTech Product Leadership Forum for cross-agency networks
- Provide portfolio management and resource allocation training

### Rotation History (past 3 years)

Business Analyst, MOH Digital Services → Product Manager, GovTech LifeSG.
- Healthcare data analytics and population health insights
- Shifted mindset from feature delivery to outcome measurement
- Developed analytical skills and healthcare policy understanding
- Transition from analysis to product ownership
- Developed stakeholder management and strategic thinking
- Moved from reactive analysis to proactive product strategy

### Span of Control upon Promotion

3 Direct Reports — 2 reportees one grade lower (Grade I reporting to Grade J). Management experience: 4-month acting role successfully guiding team through Benefits Calculator launch while maintaining delivery quality and team morale.

### Business Case

**Organisational Need:** LifeSG expanding from 8 to 15+ services over 18 months, serving 2.8M users across critical life moments. Requires Senior PM leadership for complex multi-agency coordination and product quality at scale.

**Job Description:** Lead product strategy for LifeSG's expanding service portfolio, drive cross-agency collaboration for integrated citizen experiences. Responsible for roadmap alignment with national digital objectives, team development, and measurable citizen service improvements. Requires senior stakeholder management, strategic planning, and product team leadership at Grade J level.

**Strategic Alignment:** Aligns with LifeSG's critical expansion phase requiring proven multi-agency coordination and citizen-focused product development capabilities.

### Why this case works

It is honest about gaps (does not pretend they don't exist), it ties every claim to a number, it shows the officer is *already* operating at the target level (not aspirationally), and the business case ties the promotion to a real organisational need rather than personal advancement. When coaching a manager on a promotion case, push them toward this template.

## 4. Bad vs. Good Feedback (Stakeholder/Peer Feedback)

This is the canonical example for officers gathering 360-feedback or for managers reviewing what good feedback looks like.

### Bad Feedback Example

> "Alex is a great team player and always delivers good work. He's very professional and easy to work with. He communicates well and is always willing to help. I think he should continue doing what he's doing. Maybe he could be more proactive sometimes."

Why this fails:
- Vague generalisations with no specific examples
- No concrete situations or timeframes
- Generic praise that could apply to anyone
- Unhelpful development suggestion with no context
- No measurable outcomes or impact

### Good Feedback Example (using STAR)

**Situation:** Alex was responsible for conducting user research and redesigning the onboarding flow to improve completion rates.

**Task:** Designed and led the discovery and redesign of the onboarding experience.

**Action:**
- Conducted 15 user interviews
- Created detailed user journey maps
- Identified three critical friction points in existing process
- Designed and tested five different prototypes
- Involved stakeholders from customer success and engineering in iteration process

**Result:**
- New onboarding flow increased completion rates from 60% to 85%
- Generated 200 additional successful onboardings per month
- Research methodology adopted by two other product teams
- Project delivered two weeks ahead of schedule

**Development area:** Earlier stakeholder alignment needed. Customer success had valuable insights that could have shaped research questions from the start, potentially saving a week of iteration time.

### Why the good example works

It uses the STAR structure throughout, every claim is countable, the development area is specific and actionable rather than the generic "be more proactive", and you cannot copy-paste this feedback onto another officer — it could only describe Alex.

## 5. The 4-Step Manager Evaluation Write-Up Process

When a manager is staring at a blank page for an officer's write-up, walk them through this process from the Playbook rather than diving straight into prose.

**Step 1 — Comprehensive Work Inventory.** List everything the officer worked on chronologically — major projects, BAU work, problem-solving, process improvements, collaboration, learning, and external engagement. Don't filter yet. The point is to surface things you'd otherwise forget (this fights Recency bias).

**Step 2 — Prioritise & Structure.** Select the Top 3–5 "Big Rocks" — contributions with measurable organisational impact, significant leadership, or growth beyond their previous capabilities. Everything else gets compressed or cut.

**Step 3 — Structure Your Narrative.** Arrange contributions to show the most impactful work first. Evaluate each by Scope, Scale, Complexity, Innovation, Strategic Alignment, and Stakeholder Value. The opening lines of the write-up are what the calibration panel reads first; do not bury the headline.

**Step 4 — Apply the 3S Writing Principle.** Write every statement to be Simple (clear language), Sharp (specific metrics), and Succinct (Outcome–Action–Impact structure, 1–2 sentences per bullet).

When a manager submits a draft and asks for review, audit it against this 4-step process. Most weak write-ups skipped Step 1 (so they have Recency bias baked in) or Step 2 (so they read as a flat list rather than a ranked narrative).

## 6. The Panel Stress-Test Checklist

Before a write-up goes to calibration, run it through these six tests. Any failure is a rewrite.

1. **Specificity** — Are claims backed by specific numbers or measurable outcomes?
2. **Attribution** — Is the officer's role in this work crystal clear? (Not shared credit or ambiguous ownership.)
3. **Level fit** — Would a sceptical panel member believe this is achievable at the officer's level?
4. **Vague-language scrub** — Have you avoided "strong", "effective", or "good"?
5. **Outcome–Action–Impact structure** — Does each statement follow it?
6. **Independent verifiability** — Have you provided evidence that can be independently verified?

When reviewing a draft, call out which test it fails by name. "This bullet fails the attribution test — it says 'led the team' but four people share that claim. What was your specific contribution?" is more useful than "make this stronger".

## 7. The Activity Trap (the single most common writing failure)

Every weak write-up the Playbook shows has the same root cause: the officer described **what they did** rather than **what changed because of what they did**. The Playbook calls this the Activity Trap.

A useful diagnostic when reviewing a draft:

| If the bullet starts with… | …it's probably Activity, not Impact |
|---|---|
| "Conducted…" | What changed? |
| "Managed…" | What was different at the end of the management? |
| "Helped with…" | What was your specific contribution and what did it produce? |
| "Worked on…" | What did the work achieve? |
| "Was responsible for…" | What did the responsibility produce? |
| "Held meetings about…" | What decisions came out of the meetings? |

Push the writing one level up the Results Chain. Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact. If the bullet is at Activity, ask "what was the output?". If it's at Output, ask "what was the outcome?". If it's at Outcome, ask "what was the impact on users / the team / the organisation?". Most strong bullets sit at Outcome and gesture at Impact.

## 8. Vague vs. Sharp Examples (single-line edits)

These short pairs are useful when a manager is editing line-by-line and wants quick before/after illustrations of Sharpness.

| Vague | Sharp |
|---|---|
| "Implemented a new filter" | "Created a filter that reduced citizen services turnaround by 85%" |
| "Created a filter in the website" | "15% increase in applications after launch of new filter" |
| "10 meetings held" | "Alignment achieved on 5 key decisions across 10 meetings" |
| "Improved efficiency" | "Reduced processing time by 40%" |
| "Strong stakeholder management" | "Unified 3 competing stakeholder groups" |

Each fix is the same move: replace the activity or adjective with the outcome it produced.
