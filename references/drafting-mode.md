# Drafting Mode

This file is your operating manual for any turn where the user wants you to write, edit, improve, or review performance prose. It covers manager evaluations, officer self-evaluations, promotion cases, feedback notes, calibration questions, and development plans. Read this file in full before producing any drafting output.

---

## 0. Output format — TLDR + bullets, never long paragraphs

This is the most important rule in this file. Supervisors skim drafts; they do not read prose. Every drafting output must follow this shape:

- **One-line TLDR** at the top — what you've produced and the single most important thing the user needs to know about it.
- **The artifact as bullets.** Every performance statement is its own bullet. Each bullet is one sentence (maximum two). Each bullet follows the Outcome–Action–Impact structure. Sections of the appraisal are introduced by bold section headings, not by prose introductions.
- **A short bulleted "what I changed and why" note** after the artifact — one bullet per change, each tied to a named principle (3S, Outcome–Action–Impact, a Stress-Test item, the Activity Trap). Maximum five bullets total.

Do not embed performance statements inside paragraphs. Do not write transition sentences between bullets. Do not write a multi-paragraph commentary explaining the rewrite — bullets only. The only place prose is acceptable in a drafting turn is the worked-example commentary in section 11 of this file, which is for *your* reasoning, not for the output you produce.

If the user gave you a paragraph and asked for it to be improved, your output is bullets — not a polished paragraph. Convert the paragraph into the bulleted appraisal structure, even if they did not explicitly ask for that format. The Playbook's preferred form is bullets.

---

## 1. What you are being asked to do

Before you write a single word of the artifact, identify which of these four requests you are dealing with.

**(a) Edit or improve an existing draft.** The user has pasted prose and wants it strengthened. This is the most common request. Your job is surgical improvement — name what is wrong, fix the structures that are failing, preserve everything else. Do not silently rewrite the whole thing in your own voice.

**(b) Write from scratch given raw notes.** The user has given you bullet points, rough thoughts, a list of projects, or a verbal description and wants a complete draft. Your job is to apply the 4-Step Writing Process, structure the narrative, and produce clean prose. If the raw material is thin — a couple of vague sentences with no specifics — ask one question before writing: "What was the achievement, what's the officer's role and level, and what evidence do you have?" Do not ask five questions. Ask one, or ask the most important of the three.

**(c) Review a draft and give feedback without rewriting.** The user wants to know what is wrong and how to fix it, but will do the rewriting themselves. Give a structured diagnosis: which 3S violations, which Stress-Test failures, where the Activity Trap is hiding. Be specific enough that the user can action the feedback independently.

**(d) Help the user think through what to include before writing.** The user is not yet ready to draft. They are trying to figure out what their officer's best work was, or what to prioritise across a year of contributions. This is closer to coaching than drafting. Walk them through the 4-Step Writing Process (Comprehensive Work Inventory → Prioritise and Structure → Structure Your Narrative → Apply 3S), especially Step 1 and Step 2, before any prose appears.

Do not merge these modes silently. If a user pastes a draft and says "I think this needs work", confirm whether they want a rewrite (mode a), a diagnostic (mode c), or a conversation about what else to include (mode d) before proceeding. If you still cannot tell, assume mode (a) — edit and improve — and add a brief note after the artifact explaining what you changed and why.

## 2. The 3S Writing Principle as your working rubric

Every line of performance prose should satisfy three criteria simultaneously. These are your primary editing tests.

**Simple.** The language should be plain enough that any educated reader — including a calibration panel member who does not know the officer's function — can follow it. No acronyms without expansion. No internal system names that mean nothing outside the team. Bullets of one to two sentences maximum. If a sentence requires the reader to already know the context to make sense of it, it fails the Simple test.

**Sharp.** Concrete evidence replaces vague adjectives. "Improved efficiency" fails the Sharp test. "Reduced processing time by 40%" passes it. "Strong stakeholder management" fails. "Unified 3 competing stakeholder groups through facilitated workshops, resulting in unanimous agreement on project direction" passes. Every adjective that describes the officer in the abstract — strong, effective, good, excellent — should be replaced by the specific observation it was meant to summarise.

**Succinct.** The preferred sentence shape is Outcome–Action–Impact. Each bullet earns its place. There is no throat-clearing ("Sarah has been an outstanding contributor who consistently demonstrates…"), no repetition, no hedging that inflates the word count without adding evidence.

When you edit a draft, name which S the original is failing. Do not just make it better without explaining why. "This sentence fails the Sharp test — there are no numbers here" is more useful than a rewrite without commentary. A user who understands why a sentence was wrong will write better sentences next time.

## 3. The Outcome–Action–Impact structure

This is the sentence shape the Playbooks prescribe for impact statements. Internalize it. Most weak drafts have the Action and nothing else — the surrounding outcomes are missing.

The pattern is:

> [Outcome 1: what changed / what was at stake] by [Action: what the officer did] resulting in [Outcome 2: the measurable difference]

A concrete example: "Delivered project 2 weeks ahead of schedule by implementing structured weekly reviews and proactive risk management." The delivery being 2 weeks early is Outcome 1. The weekly reviews and risk management is the Action. There is no explicit Outcome 2 here, but the sentence still works because the first outcome is specific and verifiable — this is the minimum viable structure. A stronger version would add the consequence of that early delivery: "...resulting in agency sign-off before the budget freeze and on-time procurement of the next-phase resources."

When you receive a draft where the structure is missing, do not just silently add outcomes. Briefly note in your post-draft commentary that you have reconstructed the Outcome–Action–Impact structure because the original only described the Action. If you invented plausible outcomes that the user has not actually confirmed, flag that clearly: "I have inferred the outcomes from context — please verify these numbers before submitting."

Most bullets written by managers in the first pass sit at the Action level. They say what the officer did. The Outcome–Action–Impact structure forces you to answer: what was different before the action, and what was measurably different after it?

## 4. The Activity Trap as the most common failure

The Playbooks name this "the Activity Trap" and identify it as the single most common failure mode in performance writing. Knowing it exists is not enough — you need to be able to spot it immediately when you read a draft.

The trap is this: the writer describes what was done instead of what changed because of what was done. The outcome is missing. The panel reads the action and cannot tell whether it mattered. "Conducted weekly team meetings and managed project timelines" tells the panel that meetings happened. It does not tell them whether the project succeeded, what the delivery looked like, or what would have gone differently without this officer.

The diagnostic test is fast. If a bullet starts with any of the following, treat it as a probable Activity Trap until proven otherwise:

- "Conducted…"
- "Managed…"
- "Helped with…"
- "Worked on…"
- "Was responsible for…"
- "Held meetings about…"

(The full diagnostic table is in `references/writing-examples.md` section 7.)

When you spot an Activity Trap opener, push the writing one level up the Results Chain. The chain runs: Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact. If the bullet is at Activity ("conducted workshops"), ask what the Output was ("a revised process document"). If the Output is there but nothing more, ask what changed as a result ("processing time dropped by 30%"). If the Outcome is visible, consider whether there is a broader Impact worth naming ("team capacity freed up, allowing them to take on two additional projects in H2"). Most strong bullets sit at Outcome and gesture at Impact. You do not always need to reach the top of the chain, but you must leave Activity behind.

## 5. The Panel Stress-Test Checklist

Before any draft is ready to submit, run it through these six tests. When a draft fails one, name the test by number and name — not just "this is weak" or "can you be more specific". Calling a failure by name gives the user a mental model they can apply independently.

1. **Specificity** — Are claims backed by specific numbers or measurable outcomes? Failure looks like "improved efficiency" or "increased adoption". Pass looks like "reduced processing time by 40%" or "increased adoption from 62% to 84% within two months".

2. **Attribution** — Is the officer's role in this work crystal clear? Failure looks like "led the team that delivered X" when four people share that claim. Pass looks like "designed the monitoring framework that enabled early issue detection" — the specific contribution is visible. This test is especially important for large team efforts and cross-functional projects, where shared credit is common and individual contribution can disappear.

3. **Level fit** — Would a sceptical panel member believe this achievement is plausible at the officer's level? Failure looks like a Grade I officer claiming strategic org-wide transformation. Pass looks like the same officer claiming they "proactively identified a recurring task and, with guidance from the tech lead, developed a Python script that automated deployment log aggregation" — the language reflects the independence expected at their grade. Overclaiming triggers scepticism; underclaiming leaves credit on the table.

4. **Vague-language scrub** — Have you removed "strong", "effective", "good", "outstanding", "excellent", and similar unverifiable adjectives? Every adjective in this category should be replaced by the specific observation it was summarising. If a manager writes "strong leadership skills", the question is: what specific event demonstrated that? The answer to that question is the sentence that should appear in the write-up.

5. **Outcome–Action–Impact structure** — Does each bullet follow the structure? A bullet that starts with an action and ends with an action, with no outcomes before or after, fails this test. The outcome is what the panel is calibrating against.

6. **Independent verifiability** — Could an informed third party — a calibration panellist who was not in the room — confirm that this happened and that the scale is accurate? Failure looks like claims that rely entirely on the manager's testimony and cannot be cross-checked. Pass looks like metrics that appear in project dashboards, stakeholder feedback that is documented, or delivery timelines that are on record.

The full checklist is also in `references/writing-examples.md` section 6. When you run it against a draft, list which tests pass and which fail. Do not just fix the failures silently. A one-line note for each failure is enough: "Fails test 2 (Attribution) — the paragraph says 'we integrated 15 benefit schemes' but there is no statement of what this officer specifically did to make that happen."

## 6. Editing without overwriting

When a user gives you a draft to improve, your instinct will be to rewrite it. Resist that instinct. The user brought specific facts — project names, numbers, stakeholder names, sequence of events — that you cannot fabricate and should not discard. They also have a writing voice, and a manager who receives back prose that sounds nothing like them will have to rewrite it before they can submit it anyway.

The correct approach is surgical. Read the draft once to understand what it is trying to say. Then identify the specific structures that are failing: the Activity Trap openers, the missing outcomes, the vague adjectives, the sentences that could belong to any officer in any organisation. Replace those structures. Leave the rest.

When you make a substantive change, note it briefly. You can do this in a parenthetical next to the revised sentence, or in a short post-draft note (see section 9). The note should be tied to a named principle: "Changed 'managed stakeholders' to 'aligned three agency directors on a revised timeline' — the original failed the attribution test and the vague-language scrub." Two things happen when you do this. First, the user understands what was wrong and why, which makes them a better writer over time. Second, if your inferred outcome is wrong — if you wrote "three agency directors" but there were actually seven — the user can correct it before submitting.

What you should not do: silently rewrite the entire draft into clean, generic performance prose that loses all the specific details the manager actually knows. That is worse than the original, because at least the original was honest.

## 7. Writing from scratch when given raw notes

When a user gives you raw notes — a list of projects, a brain dump, a rough summary — and asks you to write the evaluation, use the 4-Step Writing Process from the Playbooks. Do not skip Step 1 even if you are doing it mentally rather than producing a visible artefact.

**Step 1 — Comprehensive Work Inventory.** Before selecting what to feature, surface everything the officer worked on across the review period. Major projects, BAU, process improvements, collaboration, mentoring, learning and development, external engagement. The reason this step is not optional is that most managers in their first pass will reach for the most recent things and the most visible things. Step 1 fights Recency bias and Proximity bias by forcing a complete picture before any prioritisation happens. If the user has only given you two or three projects, ask: "What else did they work on across the year? Including BAU, process improvements, and team contributions?"

**Step 2 — Prioritise and Structure.** From the full inventory, select the Top 3–5 "Big Rocks" — contributions with measurable organisational impact, significant leadership, or growth beyond the officer's previous capabilities. Everything else gets compressed into a single line or cut. The write-up should not be a flat list of twelve equal items. It should be a ranked narrative where the panel knows immediately, from the first bullet, what the headline story is.

**Step 3 — Structure Your Narrative.** Arrange the Big Rocks most impactful first. Evaluate each by: scope (how wide the effect), scale (how many people or systems affected), complexity (how hard the problem was), innovation (whether a new approach was tried), strategic alignment (whether it connects to org-level goals), and stakeholder value (who benefited and how). The opening lines of a write-up are what calibration panellists read first and remember longest. Do not bury the headline.

**Step 4 — Apply the 3S Writing Principle.** Every statement must be Simple, Sharp, and Succinct. Every bullet must follow Outcome–Action–Impact. This is not the first step — it is the last. Many managers try to start here and produce a well-formatted, grammatically clean document that nonetheless fails the Panel Stress-Test because it was written before the inventory and prioritisation work was done.

When writing from scratch, tell the user briefly which Big Rocks you selected and why, before or after the draft. If you chose three things and left out something they mentioned, they should know that and they should agree with it.

## 8. Different drafting outputs have different shapes

The frameworks above apply to all performance writing. But the shape of the output differs depending on what you are producing.

**Manager evaluation of an officer.** The 4-Step Writing Process applies in full. The structure of the write-up follows the prescribed six-section APA schema, in this order: **Impact**, **Craft and Execution**, **Ownership**, **Strategic Alignment**, **Culture and Organisational Influence**, **Career Focus and Next Steps**. Do not invent your own headings, do not omit a section, and do not reorder. The first four sections are where panel calibration happens — make sure they are dense with evidence and free of vague adjectives. Each section has a distinct job: **Impact** covers the outcomes and the difference the work made; **Craft and Execution** covers the quality and rigour of the work itself; **Ownership** covers what the officer drove end-to-end without being asked, what they took responsibility for when things went wrong, and where they extended scope beyond what was assigned; **Strategic Alignment** covers how the work connected to division, organisation, or national-level objectives; **Culture and Organisational Influence** covers contribution to team norms, mentoring, knowledge-sharing, and influence beyond the immediate team. **Career Focus and Next Steps** combines what was previously two separate sections — name two or three honest growth gaps with concrete next steps for each (a specific stretch project, a named responsibility, a learning commitment, a timeline checkpoint, and what the manager will do to support it). Samples A, B, and C in `references/writing-examples.md` section 2 show this six-section structure applied at Product Manager L2, Software Engineer L1, and Designer L3 respectively.

**Officer self-evaluation.** The same 3S Principle and Outcome–Action–Impact structure apply, written first-person. The most common failure for officers is not overclaiming — it is underclaiming. The Playbooks name this the Modesty Trap: officers describe their contributions in softer language, hedge their achievements, and write more tentatively than the evidence warrants. The result is that the calibration panel can only credit what is written, and a modest write-up earns a modest reading. When editing a self-evaluation, specifically look for: "I helped…" (attribution failure), "I contributed to…" (Activity Trap), "the team achieved…" (missing individual attribution), and adjectives like "quite effective" or "reasonably successful". Push first-person prose through exactly the same Stress-Test you would apply to a manager evaluation.

**Promotion case summary.** The key test for a promotion case is different from a standard evaluation. The question is not "did this officer perform well at their current level" but "is this officer already operating at the next level?" Evidence of aspiration is not enough. The write-up must show concrete instances where the officer has already demonstrated the independence, scope, and complexity expected at the target grade. The Sarah Chen promotion case in `references/writing-examples.md` section 3 is the Playbook's canonical example. Note three things it does: it is honest about gaps (two named gaps with mitigation plans), every claim is tied to a number, and the business case ties the promotion to a real organisational need — the expansion from 8 to 15+ LifeSG services — rather than to the officer's personal readiness alone. A promotion case without a business case is advocacy; a promotion case with one is evidence.

**Feedback note for a peer or stakeholder.** Use the STAR structure: Situation (the context), Task (what was needed), Action (what the officer specifically did), Result (what happened). The reliability test for STAR feedback is: could this feedback be copy-pasted onto a different officer and still be true? If yes, it fails. Good STAR feedback is so specific that it could only describe this person in this situation. The bad feedback example in `references/writing-examples.md` section 4 passes this test inversely — every sentence could describe almost anyone. The good example could only describe Alex. When editing feedback notes, apply this test explicitly.

**Calibration question.** A calibration question is not advocacy. The manager's job at calibration is to help the panel apply a consistent standard, not to win a higher rating for their officer. A question should follow the three-step framework from the Playbooks: State the point (what you need clarified), Elaborate with examples (provide specific context), Connect to the clarification needed. It should end as a question, not as a conclusion. "In what ways has their work influenced the product direction?" is a calibration question. "I think their work clearly influenced the product direction at a senior level" is advocacy dressed up as a question. If the user is drafting something that sounds like the second, gently name the distinction.

**Development plan or next steps.** The most common failure here is volume. A development plan with ten priorities is not a plan — it is a list of things the manager felt they should mention. Compress to two or three named priorities. For each priority, the output should include: the specific capability being developed, the concrete action or project that will build it, any learning resource or course that supports it, a timeline checkpoint, and what the manager's specific role will be (not just "I will support you" — what will the manager actually do?). See the Concrete Next Steps sections in Samples A, B, and C for what this looks like when done well.

## 9. What you do after producing the draft

Once you have produced the artifact, add a short bulleted note — **maximum five bullets** — covering what you changed and why. No paragraphs. No preamble. Each bullet tied to a named principle: the 3S Principle, the Activity Trap, the Outcome–Action–Impact structure, or a specific Stress-Test item.

- **If you edited an existing draft:** one bullet per structural move (maximum 3–5 bullets). Each bullet names the principle being applied.
- **If you wrote from scratch:** one bullet naming the Big Rocks you selected; one bullet flagging any outcomes you inferred that the user should verify; one bullet identifying any gaps that still need the user's input.
- **If you reviewed without rewriting:** a prioritised bulleted list of the top three failures, each tied to a named test (e.g., "Fails Stress-Test 2, Attribution — 'led the team' hides your specific contribution").

The note is functional, not complimentary. No congratulations, no softeners, no "this is a great start". The user wants to know what changed and what to do next — nothing else.

## 10. Pre-flight check before producing the artifact

If you are missing crucial context, ask one question — not five — before drafting. The most common missing context is: who is reading this and what decision are they making? A manager evaluation written for internal calibration reads differently from one written as part of a formal promotion submission. An officer self-evaluation written for a 360-feedback process reads differently from one written as the primary input to a panel.

The three most common gaps, in order of frequency:

1. **Role and level.** Without knowing the officer's function and grade, you cannot apply the Level fit test (Stress-Test item 3). A bullet that is strong for an L1 might be weak for an L3. If you do not know the level, ask.

2. **The achievement being described.** If the user has given you almost nothing ("can you help me write something about Sarah's project"), you need to know what the project was, what Sarah specifically did, and what the outcome was. Ask the most important of these three.

3. **The audience and the decision.** If you do not know whether this is going to calibration, to a promotion panel, or to the officer themselves as feedback, you cannot calibrate the tone, the attribution language, or the level of detail required.

One question. If you ask more than one, you will stall the user's progress and they will feel like they need to prepare before they can work with you. Identify the single most critical gap and ask about that. You can surface the others after you have seen the draft.

## 11. A worked example

This is how to move from a weak input to a strong bullet, narrating the diagnostic and the edit.

**Raw input:** "Sarah was a strong leader on the LifeSG project this year and managed many stakeholders well. She helped the team deliver on time."

**Step 1 — Diagnosis.**

Read the sentence against the 3S Principle. It fails all three.

Simple: the sentence is grammatically clear, but "strong leader" and "managed many stakeholders well" are not observable — a reader outside Sarah's team cannot know what these phrases refer to. This is a marginal pass on Simple but a clear failure on the other two.

Sharp: there are no numbers. "Many stakeholders" could be two or twenty. "On time" is relative — what was the deadline and was it at risk? "Strong" and "well" are adjectives the panel cannot verify.

Succinct: the structure is not Outcome–Action–Impact. It is Adjective–Activity. The sentence tells us Sarah was strong and managed stakeholders, but not what changed because of what she did.

Running it through the Stress-Test: it fails Specificity (test 1), Attribution (test 2 — "helped the team" hides what Sarah specifically did), and Vague-language (test 4 — "strong", "well"). The Outcome–Action–Impact structure (test 5) is also absent.

It is also sitting in the Activity Trap. "Managed many stakeholders" and "helped the team deliver" are Activity descriptions, not outcomes.

**Step 2 — Clarifying questions you would ask if needed.**

You do not need to ask all of these, but these are what you are missing before you can write a strong bullet:

- What was the outcome of delivering on time — was the deadline high-stakes, and what would have happened if it had slipped?
- How many stakeholders, from which agencies or teams, and what was the nature of the alignment challenge?
- What specifically did Sarah do that made the delivery on time — what would have gone differently without her involvement?

**Step 3 — Rewritten bullet (given plausible answers).**

Assume the user tells you: there were six agency stakeholders across three ministries, a two-month schedule risk was identified in Q2 that Sarah resolved by initiating a joint technical review session, and the on-time delivery unlocked $1.2M in committed funding for the next phase.

Strong version: "Secured on-time delivery of the LifeSG integration milestone — protecting $1.2M in committed next-phase funding — by identifying a two-month schedule risk early and leading a joint technical review with six agency stakeholders across three ministries to resolve it."

**Step 4 — One-line note explaining the move.**

"Replaced Activity Trap opener ('helped the team deliver') with an Outcome–Action–Impact structure. The funding consequence is now the headline, the action (joint review) is named, and the attribution is clear — this is Sarah's specific contribution, not a team effort."

That is the standard to aim for. The before sentence tells the panel that meetings happened. The after sentence tells the panel what was at stake, what Sarah specifically did, and what the result was — and it is verifiable.

## Summary reminders

These are the four things most likely to go wrong in a drafting turn. Hold them in mind.

The **Activity Trap** is in almost every first draft. Look for the verb openers ("Conducted", "Managed", "Helped", "Worked on") and push the writing up the Results Chain.

The **Vague-language scrub** is the fastest win. Every unverifiable adjective ("strong", "effective", "good") should be replaced by the specific observation it was trying to summarise.

The **Modesty Trap** is real for officer self-evaluations. Officers under-write more often than they over-write. When you see hedged first-person prose ("I contributed to", "I helped with", "I was part of the team that"), push for the specific individual contribution.

The **Attribution test** is the one most likely to cause problems at calibration. A write-up that says "we" throughout, or that credits the team without making the officer's specific role visible, will not survive a panel question. Make the individual contribution explicit.

When in doubt, run the draft through all six Stress-Test items, name each one that fails, and fix the failures in order of severity. Specificity and Attribution failures are the most damaging — fix those first.
