## 1. The Three Pillars of Assessment

The core assessment lenses a manager uses to evaluate an officer's performance, and that a panel uses during calibration.

- **Impact** — What changed because of this officer's work? Direct (product/business), Indirect (through others), Positional (enabled future work).
- **Independence** — How much direction did they need? How much ambiguity did they navigate? How well did they manage up, across, and down?
- **Consistency** — Has performance been steady over the full review period? Not peaks and recent dips.

A fourth lens, **Sustainability**, is added at calibration: can they maintain this level without burnout? Do they have healthy work–life balance? Are they building sustainable practices?

## 2. The Results Chain

> Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact

- **Activity**: what you did (ran 10 meetings, held workshops)
- **Output**: what you produced (a dashboard, a PRD, a new filter)
- **Outcome**: what changed as a result of the output (process time dropped, users adopted the feature)
- **Impact**: the sustained, observable difference in users/systems/teams/outcomes (citizen trust increased, team velocity improved durably)

Panels assess on Outcome and Impact — not Activity or Output alone. The most common writing failure is stopping at Activity ("ran workshops") or Output ("delivered the dashboard") and calling it impact.

Three-step process to write an impact statement:
1. **Envision Success** — If my work is completely successful, what will be different? How does it contribute to broader organisational goals? What problems will be solved?
2. **Work Backwards from Impact** — For the envisioned success, what outcomes need to occur? What outputs must you deliver to generate those outcomes?
3. **Identify Measurable Indicators** — How will you know the impact has occurred? What evidence will demonstrate the outcomes? What data will prove success to a skeptical observer?

## 3. The Six Impact Principles

1. **Impact is about difference, not just output.** "Would this improvement likely have happened without you?" is the fundamental test. If the outcome would have occurred regardless of your involvement, it's not your impact.
2. **Impact is shaped by context.** The same achievement might be exceptional for a junior but expected for a senior. Always consider level, experience, and role expectations.
3. **Impact considers reach and difficulty.** Scales with how many people/systems are affected and how hard the problem was to solve. A well-defined problem is different from genuine ambiguity.
4. **Impact should create durable improvements.** Lasting impact — systems built, processes improved, knowledge transferred, culture shifted — is more valuable than temporary wins. Will this still matter in 6 months?
5. **Impact is layered and should scale from a solid base.** Individual excellence → team contribution → broader organisational influence. Claiming org-wide impact while struggling with basic delivery means the foundation is missing.
6. **Impact does not require high-profile projects.** Fixing a persistent bug, improving a process, mentoring a colleague, or removing a blocker can all be significant. Don't conflate visibility with value.

## 4. The 3S Writing Principle

Every statement in a performance write-up should be:

- **Simple** — plain language any educated reader can understand. No jargon. Bullets of 1–2 sentences maximum.
- **Sharp** — concrete evidence. Replace "improved efficiency" with "reduced processing time by 40%".
- **Succinct** — Outcome–Action–Impact structure. Tight. Every word earns its place.

## 5. Outcome–Action–Impact structure

The preferred sentence shape for performance statements:

> [Outcome delivered] by [specific action taken], resulting in [durable impact on users, team, or organisation].

The Outcome opens the sentence so the panel sees the result first. The Action explains how it was achieved. The Impact closes the sentence with the lasting difference the work made — the change that persists beyond the project itself.

Example: *"Reduced citizen wait times for permit approvals from 14 days to 3 days by re-architecting the approval workflow, resulting in 40% lower help-desk volume sustained over six months."*

Example: *"Delivered the new onboarding flow 2 weeks ahead of schedule by introducing structured weekly design reviews, resulting in completion rates rising from 60% to 85% and the review pattern adopted by two adjacent product teams."*

## 6. The 4-Step Writing Process

1. **Comprehensive Work Inventory** — list everything you worked on chronologically. Major projects, BAU, problem-solving, process improvements, collaboration, learning, external engagement.
2. **Prioritise & Structure** — select your Top 3–5 "Big Rocks": contributions with measurable organisational impact, significant leadership, or growth beyond previous capabilities.
3. **Structure Your Narrative** — arrange contributions most impactful first. Evaluate each by Scope, Scale, Complexity, Innovation, Strategic Alignment, and Stakeholder Value.
4. **Apply the 3S Writing Principle** — every statement Simple, Sharp, Succinct with Outcome–Action–Impact structure.

## 7. The Panel Stress-Test Checklist

Before submitting a write-up, validate against these six criteria:

1. Is my role in this work crystal clear? (Not shared credit or ambiguous ownership.)
2. Are my claims backed by specific numbers or measurable outcomes?
3. Would a skeptical panel member believe this is achievable at my level?
4. Have I avoided vague language like "strong", "effective", or "good"?
5. Does each statement follow the Outcome–Action–Impact structure?
6. Have I provided evidence that can be independently verified?

## 8. The Calibration Question Framework (4 lenses)

The lenses the panel uses and the question stems under each.

**Impact**
- What was the value delivered, and how long did it take?
- What has not landed yet but sets up future success?
- What indirect impact did they have?

**Independence**
- How much of the value was their responsibility?
- How much support and direction did they need?
- How did they manage and leverage other officers?

**Consistency**
- How often have they delivered at this level?
- Has performance been steady over time?
- Are there peaks and dips?

**Sustainability**
- Can they maintain this performance without burnout?
- Do they have healthy work–life balance?
- Are they building sustainable practices?

The panel also asks evaluator-framing questions:
- Is your report's self-evaluation accurate? How complex was the work? How independently was it executed? Did it meet, exceed, or fall short of expectations?
- Are there important factors not captured in the written evaluation? Did the officer deliver despite adversity, resource constraints, or other challenges?
- Do observations, self-eval, and peer feedback align? Do they support a consistent and confident rating?

## 9. Calibration principles and "Do's / Don'ts"

**Principles**
- "We're Here as the Leadership Team" — not to advocate for our own reports. The only "win" is clear standards consistently applied.
- "Org > Team > Individual" — when tied, decide what's best for the org first, then team, then individuals.
- "Stay Grounded in Details" — no hand-waving around "complexity" or "ambiguity". Precise and specific.
- "Own the Outcomes" — ask and discuss as long as needed to come out comfortable.

**Do's during calibration**
- Listen actively to the question being asked and respond directly
- Take notes; update evaluations based on conclusions
- Seek help from peers and panel if you need help conveying calibrated outcomes
- Work to see each other's perspectives
- State your opinion and name what you're unsure about
- Ask "what" and "how" questions

**Don'ts during calibration**
- Get defensive about your reports (calibration is not a courtroom)
- Defer to the manager or function lead without expressing your perspective
- Ask "why" questions — use "what" and "how" instead
- Bring in additional information outside of write-ups (avoid the "super calibrator" effect)
- Be vague or base answers on gut feel
- Conflate your points — address specific questions first before adding context

**Disagree and Commit** — remember: *it's you + calibrator vs. differences in standards, not you vs. calibrator.*

## 10. The Three-Step Framework for Asking Clarifying Questions (calibration)

1. **State the point** — clearly identify what you need clarification on.
2. **Elaborate with examples** — provide specific context.
3. **Connect to the clarification you need.**

Example calibration questions worth having in your pocket:
- "Aside from the two features steered away from, what other areas of ambiguity did they manage?"
- "In what ways has their work influenced the product direction?"
- "What is their level of project leadership? Do they make difficult calls to unblock the team?"
- "Could you say more about the complexity they navigated?"
- "How much support were they given and how independent were they?"

**When panel disagrees with your rating** — Ask "what" and "how" questions to understand where standards differ. Don't argue the individual case; help the panel see the evidence. If the panel's view holds, *Disagree and Commit*: update the evaluation in the system and communicate the outcome to your officer in a 1:1.

## 11. STAR Method (for writing feedback)

Used when writing feedback *about* someone — not for structuring performance conversations.

- **Situation** — the context
- **Task** — what needed to be done
- **Action** — what the person specifically did
- **Result** — what happened because of it

Use STAR to make feedback specific and observable. See `references/writing-examples.md` for the full Good vs Poor Feedback pair.

## 12. The Four Conversation Types

| Type | Duration | Frequency | Purpose |

## 13. The Five Biases to Watch

Name these when you spot the pattern in a manager's assessment. Don't accuse — ask one question to test the hypothesis.

- **Recency bias** — weighting recent events more than the full year. Mitigation: document performance patterns over time, not just recent peaks.
- **Halo / Horn effect** — one strong (or weak) dimension colours all others. Mitigation: assess each dimension independently.
- **Similarity bias** — favouring officers who communicate, think, or present like you do. Mitigation: apply the same standard to everyone.
- **Attribution bias** — crediting successes to the officer but blaming failures on circumstances (or vice versa for officers you're less bonded with). Mitigation: apply the same attribution standard across officers.
- **Proximity bias** — favouring officers you interact with most. Mitigation: don't let visibility substitute for impact.

Broader mitigations: use multiple data sources, document specific examples, calibrate with peers to surface bias, seek feedback from trusted colleagues, review your ratings over time for patterns.

## 14. The Three Types of Impact

- **Product / Business Impact** — product metrics you helped move: new features, adoption rates, cost savings, user satisfaction.
- **Team Impact** — making your immediate team more productive, effective, and capable: process improvements, scrum ceremonies, knowledge sharing.
- **Organisational Impact** — helping the wider organisation achieve goals: building capabilities in other teams, mentoring across the org, enabling other teams to deliver.

Impact is *layered* — it should scale from a solid base. An officer claiming org-wide impact while their individual delivery is shaky is missing the foundation.

## 15. Types of Targets and Types of Evidence

**Targets**
- *Absolute*: exact numbers or future states ("Reduce processing time to under 2 hours", "Migrate 8/8 agencies by Q3", "Achieve 95% user satisfaction rating").
- *Relative*: improvements from current state ("Increase efficiency by 25%", "Reduce errors by 50%", "Improve response time by 30%").

**Evidence types**
- Performance Metrics
- Financial Data
- Comparative Data
- Stakeholder Feedback
- Observational Data
- Documentary Evidence

Targets define what success looks like. Evidence proves you achieved it. Together they form the foundation of a compelling impact narrative.

**Common measurement pitfalls**
- *Measuring Activity Instead of Results* → Fix: focus on what changed, not what you did. Instead of "10 meetings held", "alignment achieved on 5 key decisions".
- *Setting Measures You Can't Influence* → Fix: ensure measures connect to things you can actually affect. Acknowledge broader factors and focus on your specific contribution.
- *Ignoring Stakeholder Perspectives* → Fix: regularly validate that your measures align with what stakeholders actually value.
- *Failing to Establish Baselines* → Fix: establish starting points so you can demonstrate change over time.

## 16. Demonstrating Impact Across Work Types

**Business-as-Usual (BAU)** — often undervalued but represents critical organisational function. Focus on the value preservation and improvement created, not the routine activities themselves. Think in terms of: Prevention Impact (what problems did your BAU work prevent?), Efficiency Impact (how did you make BAU more effective?), Quality Impact (how did you improve BAU outcomes?), Innovation Impact (what improvements did you introduce?).

**Experimental / Research Work** — outcomes are inherently uncertain. Focus on quality of approach, learning generated, and strategic value — even when experiments fail. *"Led 6-month experiment testing blockchain for secure document sharing, which revealed technical limitations and compliance challenges, saving organisation from unsuitable technology investment."* is a valid impact statement.

**Long-Term Projects** — break impact into measurable milestones while maintaining connection to ultimate objectives. Show progress against planned intermediate goals (e.g., "Completed comprehensive requirements gathering involving 50+ stakeholders, establishing shared vision and securing $1.2M funding commitment.").

**Large vs Small Projects** — impact isn't determined by project size. A small project with outsized results can demonstrate more impact than a large project with mediocre outcomes.

## 17. Function-Specific Metrics Reference

- **Software Engineers**: code quality (bug reduction, test coverage), engineering/system optimisations, reduced downtime & increased scalability, cost savings, critical incident interventions.
- **Product Managers**: product adoption & feature usage rates, customer satisfaction scores, revenue impact of product initiatives, time-to-market improvements.
- **Designers**: conversion rate & adoption improvements, usability testing quantitative scores, average time-to-complete key user journeys.
- **Engineering Managers**: team productivity metrics, innovation and delivery velocity, team satisfaction scores (pulse checks), cost optimisations.
- **All Roles (Qualitative)**: cross-functional collaboration successes, mentorship and team development, complex challenges successfully navigated, alignment of work with broader org goals.

## 18. Common Writing Pitfalls (named traps)

- **The Activity Trap** — listing what you did instead of what changed. The single most common failure mode.
- **The Copy-Paste Syndrome** — reusing last year's language. Panels notice. Pushes work into generic territory.
- **The Inflation Temptation** — overclaiming scope, impact, or independence. The Panel Stress-Test will catch it.
- **The Modesty Trap** — underclaiming. Panels can only credit what you wrote. "Quality trumps quantity — focus on your most significant and distinctive contributions."
